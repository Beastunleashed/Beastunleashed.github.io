Jack Hobbs

The nice guy...


Jack Hobbs was the face of England cricket during the early 1900s. Along with being a prolific batsmen, he was a man of honesty and intergrity. He eventually ended up with 61,237 first-class runs. He might have even scored more runs, however the Great War intervened, and also he was inclined to get out shortly after getting a 100, in order to give others a go. He played well into his 40s, spanning a long career. He became world-known when he became the first cricketer ever to be knighted.

Hobbs' father, John Cooper Hobbs, introduced him to cricket when he became a groundsman, and also umpired to Jesus college. Hobbs would field, bat, and bowl at the nets during the school holidays. He also would play his own version of cricket with the College servants, using a tennis ball, a cricket stump for a bat and a tennis post for a wicket.



This form of pratice laid the foundations of his skill. Hobbs was self-taught and never coached. However, there is one piece of advice, given to him by his father, that he always remembers. One day, when facing spin bowling from his father on Jesus College Cloase, Hobbs was inclined to stand clear of his stumps. "Don't draw away" his father told him.

Cricket became Hobbs' passion. His dream was to play for one of the leading counties, preferably Surrey, the county of his role model, Tom Hayward. And boy when he did play for Surrey, he dominated all bowlers. With a total of 199 centuries in First-Class cricket career, Jack Hobbs is undoubtedly one of the best cricketers of all time.
